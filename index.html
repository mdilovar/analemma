Equation of Time Throughout the Year

Understanding the Analemma

The analemma is the figure-8 pattern traced by the Sun in the sky when photographed at the same time each day over a year. This shape emerges from two overlapping effects that create waves of different frequencies.

Key Concept: "Early" and "Late" are Relative to Average

Every single day, Earth must rotate slightly MORE than 360° to face the Sun again (~361°, about 4 extra minutes). Our clocks are set to the average extra rotation needed.

"Sun Late" = Earth rotated MORE than average today (clock is ahead of Sun)
"Sun Early" = Earth rotated LESS than average today (clock is behind Sun)

Two Effects, Two Frequencies

● Orbital Speed (red dashed line) — 1 cycle per year
Fast at perihelion (Jan) → late. Slow at aphelion (Jul) → early.

● Axial Tilt (blue dashed line) — 2 cycles per year
The Sun's path on the tilted ecliptic "projects" onto east-west motion at varying rates. This effect peaks 4 times per year (near solstices AND equinoxes).

These two waves add together! Sometimes they reinforce (extra late/early), sometimes they cancel. That's why each loop has both early AND late regions — the two effects don't peak at the same time.

Why Does Tilt Alone Create a Figure-8? (Not Just a Vertical Line)

Even with zero eccentricity (constant orbital speed), the tilted ecliptic causes east-west variations! Here's why:

Near solstices: The Sun moves nearly parallel to the equator — all motion is east-west → Sun appears to move faster eastward → Sun runs LATE

Near equinoxes: The Sun crosses the equator at an angle — some motion goes north-south → less east-west progress → Sun runs EARLY

This creates a 2-cycle-per-year wave in the equation of time (the blue dashed line). Combined with the 1-cycle-per-year declination change, you get a symmetric figure-8!

Try this: Set eccentricity to 0 — you'll see a perfectly symmetric figure-8 from tilt alone!

Why is the Winter Loop Bigger?

Earth reaches perihelion in early January — right in the middle of the northern winter! This is when the orbital speed effect is at its maximum, pushing the Sun to its most "late" position.

In summer, Earth is near aphelion where the orbital speed effect is weakest. The tilt effect still creates a loop, but it's smaller without the strong speed contribution.

Try this: Move the "Perihelion Day" slider to day 182 (July). Watch the loops swap sizes!

Earth's Axial Tilt (23.4°) creates both the north-south motion AND contributes a 2-cycle-per-year wave to the equation of time.

Orbital Eccentricity (0.017) adds a 1-cycle-per-year wave that makes one loop bigger than the other.
